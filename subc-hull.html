01
A wet submarine. No pressure vessel.

SUBC competes in the European Human Powered Submarine Race. The sub is fully flooded — pilot is on SCUBA. There's no sealing requirement, no pressure vessel. What there is: a hull that needs to be light, stiff, hydrodynamically clean, and manufacturable by students in a university shop.

The hull subteam owns the shell, viewports, hatches, and full assembly integration across four subteams.

02
Drag minimisation through CFD iteration.

Hull geometry was developed from an airfoil-inspired cross-section and iterated using SolidWorks Flow Simulation. Each geometry change was evaluated for drag before committing to tooling.

CFD results screenshot
images/subc-cfd.jpg
Structural Design

Hull bending resistance was sized using second moment of area calculations to determine minimum wall thickness, then reinforced with tophat ribs along the latitudinal axis — essentially a ribcage — to resist flexure and add local stiffness where the hull cross-section changes. Additional local reinforcement patches were added at high-load regions.

03
Buoyancy budget.

Built from SolidWorks volume outputs + component weight logs, cross-checked by suspended weighing. Final validation done in the pool with foam buoyancy inserts — if the calculation is wrong, you know immediately.

04
Wet layup and vacuum bag GFRP.

Hull shell is fibreglass, manufactured via wet layup and vacuum bag processes. I've trained the team on both — and have spent enough time in a respirator to have strong opinions about PPE.

05
In the water, not just watching from the deck.

Pool tests are where design decisions get judged. I serve as a support diver during testing: preventing wall collisions, communicating with the pilot, handling underwater hardware fixes, and assisting pilot ingress and egress. When something doesn't work, you find out immediately — and fix it in the water.
