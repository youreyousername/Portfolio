Context
A wet submarine. Fully flooded.

SUBC competes in the European Human Powered Submarine Race. The sub is wet — fully flooded, pilot on SCUBA. There's no pressure vessel, no sealing requirement. What there is: a hull that needs to be light, stiff, hydrodynamically clean, and manufacturable by students in a university shop.

As hull team lead, I own the hull shell, viewports, hatches, and full assembly integration across four subteams.

Hydrodynamics & Geometry
Drag minimisation through CFD iteration.

Hull geometry was developed from an airfoil-inspired cross-section and iterated using SolidWorks Flow Simulation. Each geometry change was run through CFD to evaluate drag reduction before committing to mould tooling.

Key Design Decision

Tophat reinforcements were added along the latitudinal axis — essentially a ribcage — to resist bending without adding bulk. Bending resistance was validated through dogleg test samples before finalising reinforcement geometry.

Buoyancy & Weight Budget
Hand calcs, then the pool doesn't lie.

Buoyancy budget was built from SolidWorks volume outputs + component weight logs, cross-checked by suspended weighing. Final validation was done in the pool with foam buoyancy inserts — if the math is wrong, you find out immediately.

Manufacturing
Wet layup and vacuum bag GFRP.

Hull shell is fibreglass, manufactured via wet layup and vacuum bag processes. I've trained the team on both — and spent enough time in a respirator to have opinions about PPE.

Testing
In the water, not just watching from the deck.

Pool tests are where design decisions get judged. I serve as a support diver during testing: preventing wall collisions, communicating with the pilot, handling underwater hardware fixes, and assisting pilot ingress and egress. When something doesn't work, you know immediately — and you fix it in the water.
